Assessment
- Premenopausal patient of reproductive age with persistent intermenstrual spotting.
- Intermenstrual bleeding is abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) and should be evaluated using the PALM-COEIN framework.
- In a 24-year-old, common etiologies include:
  - Pregnancy-related bleeding
  - Ovulatory dysfunction/anovulatory bleeding
  - Cervical causes (cervicitis, ectropion, polyp, dysplasia)
  - Endometrial causes (polyp, submucosal fibroid)
  - Iatrogenic/hormonal bleeding (contraceptives, anticoagulants)
  - Infection
  - Less commonly coagulopathy or endometrial hyperplasia/malignancy

Recommended diagnostic plan
1. Exclude pregnancy
- Obtain urine or serum hCG if not already documented.

2. Laboratory evaluation
- CBC to assess for anemia if bleeding has been persistent or heavy.
- TSH to evaluate thyroid dysfunction as a contributor to ovulatory dysfunction.
- Prolactin if there are signs of ovulatory dysfunction, galactorrhea, menstrual irregularity, or if not already resulted.
- Consider gonorrhea/chlamydia testing if cervicitis risk, pelvic pain, postcoital bleeding, discharge, or new partner exposure.
- Consider coagulation testing only if history suggests a bleeding disorder (easy bruising, heavy menses since menarche, surgical/dental bleeding, family history).

3. Imaging / structural evaluation
- Order transvaginal pelvic ultrasound as first-line imaging to evaluate for:
  - Endometrial polyp
  - Submucosal fibroid
  - Endometrial thickness/abnormality
  - Ovarian pathology
- If transvaginal ultrasound is unrevealing and spotting persists, consider saline infusion sonohysterography or hysteroscopy for improved evaluation of intracavitary lesions.

4. Cervical evaluation
- Perform/specify pelvic exam with speculum inspection if not already done.
- Confirm cervical cancer screening is up to date.
- Evaluate for cervical lesion, polyp, ectropion, friability, or cervicitis.

Management considerations
- If hemodynamically stable and pregnancy has been excluded, symptom-directed therapy may be considered while workup is ongoing, especially if bleeding is bothersome:
  - Combined hormonal contraception if no contraindications and contraception is acceptable
  - Cyclic or continuous progestin if estrogen is not desired or contraindicated
  - Levonorgestrel-releasing IUD if contraception and bleeding control are desired
- NSAIDs may help reduce bleeding in patients with concomitant heavier flow, provided no contraindication.
- If anovulation is suspected, cycle regulation with hormonal therapy is reasonable after exclusion of pregnancy and significant structural lesions.

Contingency plan if initial workup is unrevealing
- If pregnancy testing, CBC, TSH, prolactin, and transvaginal ultrasound do not identify a cause and bleeding persists:
  - Reassess medication and contraceptive history
  - Reassess for cervical/vaginal sources of bleeding
  - Consider infection testing if not already completed
  - Escalate to sonohysterography or hysteroscopy for occult intracavitary lesions
  - Consider endometrial sampling if risk factors emerge, bleeding worsens, or symptoms persist despite initial management

Suggested order
- Transvaginal pelvic ultrasound

Clinical summary
- Young reproductive-age patient with persistent intermenstrual spotting.
- Initial evaluation should ensure pregnancy exclusion, review endocrine labs (TSH/prolactin), assess for anemia and infection as indicated, and obtain transvaginal ultrasound to evaluate structural causes.
- If no structural or endocrine cause is identified, treat symptomatically based on contraceptive goals and reassess if bleeding persists.
