Clinical assessment
- Presenting bleeding pattern: Persistent intermenstrual spotting, which qualifies as abnormal uterine bleeding (AUB) if occurring outside expected cyclic menses.
- Key characterization still needed from chart review or patient interview:
  - Age and menopausal status.
  - Date of last menstrual period and cycle regularity.
  - Duration of spotting, frequency, volume, and association with intercourse, ovulation, exercise, or medications.
  - Pregnancy possibility and contraception use.
  - Associated symptoms: pelvic pain, dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia, vaginal discharge, postcoital bleeding, systemic symptoms, galactorrhea, headaches/visual symptoms, weight change, acne/hirsutism, easy bruising.
  - Risk factors for endometrial hyperplasia/malignancy: age >=45 years, obesity, chronic anovulation/PCOS, diabetes, hypertension, tamoxifen use, Lynch syndrome/family history, nulliparity, prolonged unopposed estrogen exposure.

Potential etiologies
- Structural causes, per PALM-COEIN framework:
  - Endometrial or cervical polyp.
  - Adenomyosis.
  - Leiomyoma, especially submucosal fibroid.
  - Endometrial hyperplasia or malignancy, risk dependent on age and clinical risk factors.
- Nonstructural causes:
  - Ovulatory dysfunction, including thyroid disease, hyperprolactinemia, PCOS, hypothalamic dysfunction, perimenopause.
  - Endometrial/hemostatic disorders.
  - Iatrogenic bleeding from hormonal contraception, anticoagulants, antiplatelet agents, or other medications.
  - Pregnancy-related bleeding, which must be excluded in reproductive-age patients.
  - Cervicitis/STIs, cervical dysplasia, or cervical malignancy, especially with postcoital bleeding or abnormal screening history.

Recommended diagnostic plan
1. Imaging to evaluate structural causes
- Order transvaginal pelvic ultrasound as first-line imaging for persistent intermenstrual bleeding.
  - Include transabdominal component if needed for global pelvic assessment or if transvaginal approach is not feasible.
  - Evaluate endometrial thickness/appearance, focal endometrial lesions, intracavitary/submucosal fibroids, adenomyosis features, myometrial lesions, adnexa, and free fluid.
- If transvaginal ultrasound is unrevealing but bleeding persists, or if ultrasound suggests focal intracavitary pathology:
  - Proceed with saline infusion sonohysterography and/or diagnostic hysteroscopy for improved evaluation of endometrial polyps and submucosal fibroids.
  - Hysteroscopy allows direct visualization and potential directed biopsy or polypectomy.

2. Endometrial sampling
- Recommend endometrial biopsy if any of the following apply after chart review:
  - Age >=45 years with AUB.
  - Age <45 years with risk factors for unopposed estrogen/endometrial cancer, persistent bleeding, failed medical management, or concerning ultrasound findings.
  - Intermenstrual bleeding with significant risk factors or persistent unexplained course.
- If postmenopausal bleeding is identified, evaluate urgently with transvaginal ultrasound and/or endometrial sampling according to postmenopausal bleeding protocols.

3. Laboratory evaluation
- Confirm recent results for:
  - Pregnancy test if reproductive age.
  - CBC.
  - TSH.
  - Prolactin.
- Consider additional labs based on history:
  - Ferritin/iron studies if prolonged or heavy bleeding or anemia.
  - Coagulation evaluation if lifelong heavy bleeding, easy bruising, postpartum/surgical bleeding history, or family history of bleeding disorder.
  - Androgen/PCOS evaluation if oligomenorrhea with hyperandrogenic features.
  - STI testing for chlamydia/gonorrhea/trichomonas if clinically indicated.

Symptom management options while workup proceeds
- If pregnancy is excluded and there are no contraindications, hormonal therapy may be offered for symptom control:
  - Combined hormonal contraceptive pill/patch/ring for cycle regulation and reduction of unscheduled bleeding.
  - Progestin-only therapy if estrogen contraindicated.
  - Levonorgestrel intrauterine system if patient desires longer-term management and structural/endometrial pathology has been appropriately evaluated.
- Nonhormonal options:
  - NSAIDs may reduce menstrual bleeding and dysmenorrhea if no contraindications.
  - Tranexamic acid is generally used for heavy cyclic bleeding rather than isolated spotting and should be used cautiously based on thrombotic risk.
- Treat identified cause if found:
  - Cervicitis/STI: antimicrobial therapy per results.
  - Cervical polyp: removal if symptomatic or concerning.
  - Endometrial polyp/submucosal fibroid: hysteroscopic management if symptomatic or persistent.
  - Thyroid or prolactin abnormality: manage or refer appropriately.

Contingency plan
- If ultrasound, labs, pelvic exam, and indicated endometrial sampling are unrevealing but intermenstrual spotting persists:
  - Reassess medication/contraceptive causes and adherence patterns.
  - Consider saline infusion sonohysterography or hysteroscopy if not already performed, as small focal lesions may be missed on routine ultrasound.
  - Consider empiric hormonal cycle regulation if no contraindications and malignancy/pregnancy/infection have been excluded.
  - Follow up within 6-12 weeks to review bleeding diary, imaging, labs, and response to therapy.
- Escalate evaluation sooner for heavy bleeding, symptomatic anemia, postcoital bleeding with abnormal cervical findings, pelvic mass, severe pain, pregnancy, postmenopausal bleeding, or any concern for malignancy.
