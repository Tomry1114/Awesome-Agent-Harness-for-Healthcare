Assessment
- Reported adrenal incidentaloma on CT imaging, details pending review.
- The appropriate workup depends on imaging phenotype and clinical comorbidities:
  1. Autonomous cortisol secretion screening is generally indicated for all adrenal incidentalomas unless the patient is frail or testing would not change management.
  2. Pheochromocytoma screening is indicated when the lesion is not clearly lipid-rich benign adenoma on noncontrast CT, or when imaging characteristics are unknown; it may be omitted only if noncontrast HU is <=10 and imaging is otherwise consistent with benign adenoma.
  3. Primary aldosteronism screening is indicated if the patient has hypertension and/or unexplained hypokalemia.
  4. Androgen/sex steroid testing is generally reserved for lesions suspicious for adrenocortical carcinoma or when there are clinical signs of virilization/feminization.
- Cardiovascular comorbidities such as hypertension, diabetes, obesity, dyslipidemia, atrial fibrillation, coronary disease, heart failure, stroke, or osteoporosis/fracture history would increase the clinical relevance of mild autonomous cortisol secretion and should lower the threshold for complete biochemical evaluation and treatment discussion if abnormal.

Recommended diagnostic orders, pending confirmation in EHR
A. Hormonal evaluation
1. 1-mg overnight dexamethasone suppression test
   - Order: dexamethasone 1 mg PO once at 11 PM, followed by serum cortisol at 8 AM the next morning.
   - Also order serum dexamethasone level with the 8 AM cortisol if available, to confirm adequate dexamethasone exposure.
   - Patient instructions: take dexamethasone exactly at 11 PM; have blood drawn at approximately 8 AM the next morning. Notify clinician of current glucocorticoid use, estrogen therapy, antiseizure medications, rifampin, or other interacting drugs, as these can affect interpretation.
   - Interpretation/contingency: cortisol <=1.8 mcg/dL generally excludes autonomous cortisol secretion. Cortisol >1.8 mcg/dL suggests possible mild autonomous cortisol secretion and should prompt confirmation with ACTH, DHEA-S as appropriate, repeat/confirmatory testing when needed, and assessment of cortisol-related comorbidities. Markedly abnormal results should prompt expedited endocrine follow-up and consideration of adrenal surgery depending on imaging and clinical context.

2. Plasma free metanephrines OR 24-hour urine fractionated metanephrines/catecholamines
   - Indicated unless the CT is definitively benign lipid-rich adenoma with noncontrast HU <=10.
   - Preferred if feasible: plasma free metanephrines.
   - Patient instructions for plasma testing: draw after resting supine in a quiet setting for at least 20-30 minutes; avoid caffeine, nicotine, strenuous exercise, and acute stress before testing when possible; review medications that may cause false positives.
   - Interpretation/contingency: values >3 times the upper limit of normal are strongly suspicious for pheochromocytoma/paraganglioma and should prompt urgent endocrine review, alpha blockade planning, and avoidance of biopsy or unopposed beta blockade. Mild elevations should be repeated under optimal conditions and medication/interference reviewed.

3. Aldosterone-renin ratio
   - Indicated if the patient has hypertension and/or unexplained hypokalemia. Because patient-specific BP and potassium data were not available at time of writing, this should be ordered if either condition is confirmed.
   - Order: plasma aldosterone concentration and plasma renin activity or direct renin concentration, with calculation of aldosterone-renin ratio; include serum potassium on the same day.
   - Patient instructions/prep: correct hypokalemia before testing if possible; liberal/normal sodium intake; morning collection after patient has been up for at least 2 hours and seated for 5-15 minutes. Medication effects should be reviewed. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists and epithelial sodium channel blockers can invalidate testing and often require washout if safe. ACE inhibitors/ARBs, diuretics, beta-blockers, and central alpha agonists may affect interpretation; do not stop antihypertensives without clinician supervision.
   - Interpretation/contingency: elevated aldosterone with suppressed renin and elevated ARR should prompt confirmatory testing unless criteria for direct diagnosis are met, and adrenal vein sampling may be needed before surgery if unilateral adrenalectomy is considered.

4. Additional steroid testing if imaging suspicious for carcinoma or if symptoms suggest hormone excess
   - Consider DHEA-S, androstenedione, testosterone/estradiol, 17-hydroxyprogesterone, and/or steroid profiling if the mass is large, heterogeneous, irregular, rapidly growing, HU high, necrotic, invasive, or associated with virilization/feminization.

B. Baseline safety/metabolic labs
- Comprehensive metabolic panel or basic metabolic panel to document potassium, sodium, bicarbonate, creatinine/eGFR, calcium, and glucose.
- Hemoglobin A1c and lipid panel if not recently available, given the importance of cardiometabolic assessment in possible autonomous cortisol secretion.

C. Follow-up imaging / characterization
- Arrange dedicated adrenal-protocol CT abdomen without and with contrast with washout in 1-2 months if the initial CT did not adequately characterize the lesion, provided renal function allows contrast.
- If iodinated contrast is contraindicated, order MRI abdomen adrenal protocol with chemical-shift imaging instead.
- Imaging contingency:
  - Benign phenotype: homogeneous, lipid-rich, HU <=10, small/stable lesion generally may not need repeated imaging depending on guideline context.
  - Indeterminate lesion: dedicated adrenal imaging and endocrine follow-up.
  - Concerning features such as size >=4 cm, HU >20, heterogeneity, irregular borders, necrosis, invasion, or rapid growth should prompt multidisciplinary review with endocrine surgery/radiology and consideration of adrenalectomy.
  - Do not biopsy an adrenal lesion until pheochromocytoma has been excluded biochemically and only if biopsy would change management, typically in the setting of suspected metastasis.
